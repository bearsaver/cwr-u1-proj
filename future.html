After the Constitutional Convention, the trend of suppressing the slavery issue continued.

1820 - when Congress was forced to address the slave status of new states, they again decided to compromise, and further pushed the addressal of the issue aside

1831 - when an influx of anti-slavery petitions were presented to Congress, proslavery representatives passed a "Gag Rule", which banned the discussion of those petitions

This is an excellent example of the negative impacts of suppressing the slavery questions. Instead of addressing the clear tensions that resulted from the North-South divide, Congress opted to completely ignore them, allowing them to continue growing.

1850 - the Compromise of 1850, an omnibus bill, is passed to address tensions resulting from the admission of new Mexican territory.

1854 - the Kansas-Nebraska Act is passed, leaving the fate of the territories to popular sovereignty.

Even though this legislation attempted to suppress sectional tensions, pro- and anti-slavery advocates flooded the states to sway the vote, and bloody violence ensued. This exemplifies the consequences of compromise and circumvention when tensions are already high.

All of these compromise attempts failed to resolve tensions, and in 1861, sectional agitation boiled over, and the Civil War begun.
Overall, the Constitutional Convention began a faulty trend of suppressing the slavery question, which contributed to the buildup of sectional tension.
